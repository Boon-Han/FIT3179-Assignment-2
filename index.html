Australia Bushfires (2023)

In 2023, Australia experienced significant bushfires as the fire season was driven by unsually hot and dry conditions, especially in the northen regions like Northen Teritory, Queensland and the north side of Western Australia. According to Nasa's Fire Information for Resource Management System (FIRMS), there have been approximately 30,000 confirmed bushfires tracked.

In Queensland, bushfires destroyed at least 16 houses and over a 11,000 hectares of land were burned. In New South Wales, large fires burned over 33,000 hectares of land in places Willi Willi National Park with multiple properties destroyed and one person killed.



What causes Bushfires

Bushfires can be caused by many factors but the most common ones are:

  • High temperatures
  • Low humidity
  • Lack of recent rain
  • Abundance of dry vegetation
  • Strong winds
  • Thunderstorms

There are two types of fire ignition sources which includes:

  • Natural sources such as lightning strikes.
  • Human sources either accidently or deliberately, eg. discarded cigarettes, electrical faults, arson, etc.

Fire Radiative Power

Fire Radiative Power, or FRP for short, can be defined as the rate at which an actively burning fire emits radiative energy. FRP quantifies the amount of thermal radiation emitted by the fire, which can indicate how intense or active the fire is.

In 2023, the highest recorded FRP is on October 6 with a FRP of 834.60 Watts indicating a highly intense fire that is rapidly growing and difficult to control.

Bushfires in 2023

Some parts of Australia are prone to bushfires all year round. But there are also bushfire seasons in Australia. In Northen Australia, the bushfire season peaks in winter and spring which are the dryer seasons in Australia. In Southern Australia, bushfire season peaks in summer and autumn.
